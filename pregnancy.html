Risk Factors & Medical Considerations

HIGH ATTENTION
APLA Positive (Antiphospholipid Antibody Syndrome)
APLA is an autoimmune condition where the body produces antibodies that increase blood clotting risk. This is managed with Ecosprin (low-dose aspirin) and HCQS (hydroxychloroquine). Both are standard first-line treatment in pregnancy.
Learn more ▼

What APLA means for this pregnancy: Untreated APLA significantly increases the risk of miscarriage (especially 2nd/3rd trimester), preeclampsia, intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR), and placental insufficiency. However, with proper treatment, APLA pregnancies have a 70-80% success rate.

Why Ecosprin + HCQS work: Ecosprin (low-dose aspirin) prevents platelet aggregation and reduces clotting risk. HCQS (hydroxychloroquine) modulates immune response and reduces complement activation, which is central to APLA pathology. This combination is evidence-based and highly effective.

Monitoring during pregnancy: Platelet counts should be checked regularly (trends noted: 234 at 24w — slight declining trend, continue monitoring). Uterine artery Doppler studies and serial fetal growth scans in the 3rd trimester are standard practice. Any signs of preeclampsia (elevated blood pressure, proteinuria, headache, vision changes) require immediate evaluation.

Reassuring findings: APLA antibody testing was rechecked in January 2026 and came back negative, which is positive (some patients have transient positivity). However, treatment should continue based on clinical history and prior pregnancy losses. Reaching 25 weeks is a significant milestone given this diagnosis.

Why anemia matters in pregnancy: During pregnancy, blood volume expands by 40-50%, causing physiologic hemodilution. However, severe anemia (<10 g/dL) increases risk of preterm labor, IUGR, and maternal complications during labor. Additionally, adequate maternal iron stores are critical for fetal development and are difficult to replenish postpartum.

What the recovery means: The return to 11.3 g/dL by 24 weeks demonstrates that iron supplementation (Fexacta) is effective and the patient is compliant. This is excellent news. The dietary restrictions (no wheat, no milk) were likely contributing to the dip, but with supplementation and time, levels are recovering well.

Ongoing monitoring: Continue iron supplementation through pregnancy and postpartum (at least 12 weeks). Recheck hemoglobin every 4-6 weeks in the 3rd trimester to ensure levels remain above 10.5 g/dL. If levels drop again, consider increasing supplementation or investigating for other causes of anemia.
