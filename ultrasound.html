What each anatomical check means

The Level II anatomy survey is a comprehensive checklist of all major fetal structures. Each is evaluated for proper size, position, and function:

  • Head, face, lips, spine: Check for neural tube defects, cleft lip and palate, and spinal issues like spina bifida.
  • Heart (4-chamber): Assess cardiac chambers and septa for congenital heart defects.
  • Stomach, kidneys, bladder: Check for proper organ development and function.
  • Limbs: Verify all long bones are present, properly positioned, and of normal length.
  • Placenta and cord: Assess placental location (posterior placement away from cervical os is ideal), cord insertion, and umbilical artery flow.
  • Cervical length: At 18 weeks, normal is typically 3.5–4.5 cm. The 3.59 cm measurement indicates low risk of preterm delivery.

About biometry

BPD, HC, FL, AC are plotted on standardised growth curves. Measurements consistent with 18–19 weeks confirm dating and show normal growth velocity. EFW of 256 g at the 38th centile is ideal — not too big, not too small.

Regarding maternal fatty habitus

In patients with higher BMI or fatty abdominal tissue, ultrasound penetration can be reduced, making some fetal views (especially deep cardiac structures) harder to visualise. The follow-up scan at 28–30 weeks will confirm the same reassuring findings with clearer views, particularly to rule out subtle cardiac issues.

Next steps

The growth scan at 28–30 weeks will confirm continued normal development, assess amniotic fluid volume, and monitor placental position and blood flow patterns.

What each finding means

Nuchal translucency (NT): The thickness of tissue at the back of the baby's neck, measured at 11–14 weeks. NT ≤ 3.5 mm is considered normal. The 1.10 mm measurement is excellent and significantly below the threshold. Thickened NT is associated with increased risk of Down syndrome (Trisomy 21); a normal NT substantially reduces this risk.

Nasal bone: A visible nasal bone is another reassuring marker. Absent or hypoplastic nasal bone can be associated with chromosomal abnormalities.

Tricuspid valve and ductus venosus: Cardiac Doppler assessments look at blood flow patterns. Abnormal patterns can suggest chromosomal issues. Findings here are normal.

Combined risk assessment: The NT scan is typically combined with maternal serum screening (the double marker test) to calculate overall risk. The excellent NT means that even without serum markers, the risk of trisomies is substantially reduced. With the double marker results, the combined risk is extremely low.
