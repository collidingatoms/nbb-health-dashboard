Expert Note
Summary: This is the most important screening scan. Nuchal Translucency of 1.10mm is well within normal limits (< 3.5mm). Combined with visible nasal bone, normal tricuspid valve, and normal ductus venosus — these findings significantly reduce the risk of chromosomal abnormalities including Down syndrome.

What each finding means:

Nuchal Translucency (NT): This is the thickness of the tissue at the back of the baby's neck measured at 11–14 weeks. An NT ≤ 3.5mm is considered normal. Your baby's measurement of 1.10mm is excellent and significantly below the threshold. Thickened NT is associated with increased risk of Down syndrome (Trisomy 21), but a normal NT substantially reduces this risk.

Nasal bone: The presence of a visible nasal bone is another good marker. An absent or hypoplastic nasal bone can be associated with chromosomal abnormalities. Your baby's clearly visualized nasal bone is reassuring.

Tricuspid valve and ductus venosus: These cardiac Doppler assessments look at blood flow patterns. Abnormal patterns can suggest chromosomal issues. Your baby's normal findings are very reassuring.

Combined risk assessment: The NT scan is typically combined with maternal serum screening (the double marker test done 1 day later) to calculate an overall risk for chromosomal abnormalities. Your excellent NT measurement means that even without the serum markers, the risk of Down syndrome and other trisomies is significantly reduced. When combined with your double marker results, the risk is extremely low.

What each anatomical check means:

The Level II anatomy survey is a comprehensive checklist of all major fetal structures. Each structure is evaluated for proper size, position, and function:

  • Head, face, lips, spine: Check for neural tube defects, cleft lip/palate, and spinal issues like spina bifida.
  • Heart (4-chamber): Assess cardiac chambers and septa for congenital heart defects.
  • Stomach, kidneys, bladder: Check for proper organ development and function.
  • Limbs: Verify all long bones are present, properly positioned, and of normal length.
  • Placenta and cord: Assess placental location (posterior placement away from cervical os is ideal), cord insertion, and umbilical artery flow.
  • Cervical length: At 18 weeks, normal cervical length is typically 3.5–4.5cm. Your 3.59cm measurement is perfect and indicates low risk of preterm delivery.

About biometry: The measurements (BPD, HC, FL, AC) are plotted on standardized growth curves. Your baby's measurements are consistent with 18–19 weeks gestation, confirming your dating and showing normal growth velocity. The estimated fetal weight of 256g at the 38th percentile is ideal — not too big, not too small.

Regarding maternal fatty habitus: In patients with a higher BMI or fatty abdominal tissues, ultrasound penetration can be slightly reduced, making some fetal views (especially deep cardiac structures) harder to visualize optimally. This is why a follow-up scan at 28–30 weeks was recommended — to confirm the same reassuring findings and have clearer views if needed, particularly to rule out any subtle cardiac issues.

Next steps: A growth scan at 28–30 weeks will confirm continued normal development, assess amniotic fluid volume, and monitor placental position and blood flow patterns.
